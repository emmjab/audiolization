GSAF report reveals gender gap also a problem in shark maulings

Earlier this week, the Global Shark Attack File released its annual findings of every single brutal shark mauling since 1914 — likely in the spirit of festivity for Discovery’s beloved Shark Week.

Unlike previous years, however, GSAF realized a disturbing trend. For every man sharks eat, they attack a measly .11 woman — a phenomenon experts are calling, “Like, a weird, twice-removed cousin of the wage gap.”

“Why are sharks still discriminating?” marine biologist Katie Current said. “Don’t they know gender is a protected class? The report honestly brought up more questions than answers.”

Among the mysteries with which experts must contend are the strange patterns in shark-related violence that vary by decade.

With a strange blip in data that can only be attributed to 1967’s summer of love, the late ‘60s enjoyed a noticeable lull in maulings, with annual global attacks dropping “hecka percent,” according to mathematician Algae Bra, who’s been watching Shark Week every year since like, 2003.

The sharpest uptick in shark attack data, however, has occurred in the past 15 years.

“Back in the good old days of 1915, there were merely six annual shark attacks worldwide — compared to 116 after the advent of the internet, the rise of millennials and other important factors,” Current said.
